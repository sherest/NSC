Behavioral Emergencies

Assessing Behavioral Emergencies

  • Perform the standard assessment.
  • Assess mental status by observing:

      • - General appearance

      - Posture and gait

      - Actions

      - Speech

      - Orientation for time, person and place

      - Mood, thought processes, perception, judgment, memory and attention

  • Assess potential for violence:

      • - Check scene for potential weapons.

      - In the history, ask family members and bystanders about any history of aggression or combativeness.

      - Observe patient’s posture: whether in a position that threatens self or others (clenched fists, holding lethal objects, tense muscles).

      - Observe what patient says and how: whether yelling or verbally threatening harm to self or others or seems to be hallucinating or yelling at someone not present.

      - Consider the warning signs for suicide.

      - Observe patient’s physical activity: Watch for movement toward you and quick, irregular movements.

      - Observe patient’s emotional mood, such as being agitated, uncontrollably elated, depressed, uncontrollably angry or kicking or throwing things.

      - Consider whether patient is manifesting bizarre thinking and behavior, such as hallucinations or paranoia.

Emergency Care for Behavioral Emergencies

  1. Protect the patient and yourself.
  2. Perform standard patient care.
  3. Do not leave patient alone.
  4. Consider need for law enforcement personnel. Be prepared to leave the scene to maintain personal safety.
  5. Give any medications or drugs you find to arriving EMS personnel.
  6. Do not assume the patient is intoxicated, using drugs, or otherwise impaired, because the behavior may result from a medical condition. Even if you smell alcohol, do not assume a problem is due only to intoxication.
  7. If possible, reduce any distressing stimuli at the scene.
  8. Always try to talk the patient into cooperation.
  9. Call for additional help if needed, including law enforcement if appropriate.
  10. Avoid restraints unless necessary.
  11. Calm and reassure the patient and establish rapport:
    • Approach slowly but purposefully.
    • Identify yourself and let the patient know you are there to help.
    • Ask for the patient’s name, and use it when speaking to him or her.
    • Inform patient what you are about to do.
    • Ask questions in a calm, reassuring voice.
    • In cultures where it is acceptable, make good eye contact, and stay at the patient’s eye level.
    • Without being judgmental, encourage the patient to tell what happened and what is troubling him or her.
    • Show you are listening by rephrasing or repeating part of what the patient says.
    • Acknowledge the patient’s feelings and restate that you are there to help.
    • Maintain a comfortable distance, and avoid unnecessary physical contact. Do not make quick moves. Avoid any posture that might seem threatening.
    • Respond honestly to the patient’s questions; do not lie to the patient.
    • Do not belittle, threaten, challenge, or argue with disturbed patients.
    • Do not “play along” with visual or auditory hallucinations, but do not challenge them.
    • Involve trusted family members or friends in care activities.
    • Be patient and prepared to stay at scene for a long time.
    • Always remain with the patient.
  12. Call for additional help if needed.
