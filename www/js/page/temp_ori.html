RESPIRATORY EMERGENCIES

Signs and Symptoms of Respiratory Distress

  • Gasping for air  
  • Panting  
  • Breathing faster or slower than normal  
  • Wheezing or other sounds with breathing  
  • Using accessory muscles  
  • Inability to speak a full sentence without pausing to breathe  
  • Skin is pale, cool, and moist; lips and nail beds are bluish (cyanosis)
  • Dizziness or disorientation  
  • Feelings of extreme distress  
  • Sitting and leaning forward, hands on knees

Inadequate Breathing

  • Inadequate breathing is breathing too slowly (less than 10 breaths/minute) or too weakly.
  • Patient likely has altered mental status and may have cyanotic appearance.
  • Patient needs ventilations.
  • Do not wait for respiratory arrest before providing ventilations for an adult who is not breathing adequately.
  • An infant or child may have a pulse of 60 or higher but still be breathing inadequately and need ventilation.

Emergency Care for Respiratory Distress

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Help the patient rest in a position of easiest breathing (often sitting up).
  3. Ask the patient about any prescribed medicine, and help the patient take it if needed.
  4. Be prepared to give BLS, because respiratory distress may progress to respiratory arrest.
  5. Follow local protocol to administer oxygen if available and you are trained.
  6. Use suction as needed to maintain the airway.
  7. Because respiratory distress in an infant or a child may rapidly progress to arrest, act quickly when an infant or a child is having problems breathing.

Emergency Care for an Asthma Attack

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Follow local protocol to assist the patient in using his or her medication (usually a metered dose inhaler) when:
  • Patient confirms it is an asthma attack.
  • Patient identifies the inhaler as his or her prescribed asthma medication.
  • Patient cannot self-administer the medication.
  1. Help the patient rest and sit in a position for easiest breathing.
  2. Patient may use an inhaler again if needed.
  3. Follow local protocol to give oxygen if available and you are trained.

See page 73 for assisting with a bronchodilator for asthma attack.

SCENE SAFETY

Scene Size-Up

  • En route to the scene, consider safety factors for you, bystanders, and the patient.
  • Dispatcher’s information may alert you to hazards or special precautions.
  • As you arrive, begin scene size-up before exiting the vehicle and while approaching the patient, including:
    • Appropriate BSI precautions to take  
    • Any hazards at the scene  
    • Clues to help you better understand the nature of the emergency

Vehicle Crash Hazards

  • Traffic hazards  
  • Downed wires  
  • Risk of fire or explosion  
  • Unstable vehicles  
  • Hazardous materials

Potential Violence

  • Crime scenes  
  • Potentially violent patient or bystanders  
  • Guard dogs, wild animals

Environmental Dangers

  • Unstable surfaces  
  • Water, ice  
  • Weather extremes

Hazards within Structures

  • Low-oxygen areas  
  • Toxic substances, fumes  
  • Risk of collapse  
  • Risk of fire or explosion

Principles of Scene Safety

  • Enter the scene only if it is safe.
  • If the scene is unsafe, make it safe or stay away.  
  • Protect bystanders and other rescuers.
  • Protect the patient from environmental threats such as temperature extremes, rain.

SEIZURES

Causes of Seizures

  • High fever  
  • Epilepsy  
  • Head injuries  
  • Poisoning  
  • Low oxygen levels  
  • Low blood sugar
  • Other causes

Emergency Care for Seizures

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Protect the patient from the environment.
  3. Loosen constricting clothing. Remove eyeglasses.
  4. Ask bystanders (except caretakers) to leave the area.
  5. Ensure that the airway remains open.
  6. Never attempt to restrain the patient.
  7. Do not put anything in the patient’s mouth.
  8. If the patient is bluish, ensure the airway is open and give ventilations.
  9. After the seizure, place an unresponsive patient in the recovery position.
  10. Seizure patients often have significant oral secretions; be prepared to suction the airway.
  11. Follow local protocol to administer oxygen if available and you are trained.
  12. Report assessment findings to additional EMS personnel.

SHOCK (HYPOPERFUSION)

Signs and Symptoms of Shock

  • Restlessness, anxiety  
  • Extreme thirst  
  • Rapid, weak pulse  
  • Rapid, shallow respirations  
  • Mental status changes  
  • Pale, cool, moist skin (or ashen-colored skin in a dark-skinned person)
  • Decreased blood pressure (a late sign)

Emergency Care for Shock

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. If the patient is bleeding, prevent further external blood loss.
  3. Position responsive patient lying on the back with legs raised such that the feet are 6 to 12 inches above the ground (unless there is head, spinal, leg, or pelvis injury). Put an unresponsive breathing patient without trauma in the recovery position.
  4. Maintain a normal body temperature. If available, put a blanket under the patient.
  5. Do not give the patient anything to eat or drink.
  6. Provide care for specific injuries.
  7. Follow local protocol to administer high-flow oxygen (15 L/min by non-rebreather mask) if available and you are trained.
  8. Continue to monitor the patient’s breathing and vital signs every 5 minutes. Be prepared to maintain an open airway in case of vomiting.

SPINAL AND HEAD INJURIES

Suspect a Head or Spinal Injury

  • With any unresponsive trauma patient  
  • When large forces were involved

Signs and Symptoms of Head or Spinal Injury

  • Lump or deformity in the head, neck, or back  
  • Changing levels of responsiveness  
  • Difficulty breathing or shallow breathing
  • Unequal pupils  
  • Headache  
  • Clear fluid from the nose or ears  
  • Stiff neck  
  • Inability to move any body part  
  • Tingling, numbness, weakness, or lack of feeling in the feet or hands  
  • Pain or tenderness in an area of injury or associated with movement  
  • Continuous or intermittent pain along the spinal column or extremities
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control

Emergency Care for Head Injuries

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Use the jaw thrust if necessary to open the airway.
  3. Follow local protocol to administer oxygen if available and you are trained.
  4. Manually stabilize the head and neck, and do not let the patient move.
  5. Closely monitor mental status.
  6. Control bleeding without putting pressure directly on a skull fracture.
  7. Dress and bandage open wounds as for soft-tissue injuries.
  8. Monitor vital signs.
  9. Be prepared for vomiting.

Emergency Care for Spinal Injuries

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Give general care as for a head injury.
  3. Maintain constant manual spinal motion restriction.
  4. Without moving the head, if possible, maintain the airway.
  5. Follow local protocol to administer oxygen if available and you are trained.
  6. Keep the head in line with the body if the patient must be positioned for ventilations or CPR.

SPLINTING

Splint an injured extremity if there is risk for movement of the area unless help is expected within a few minutes.

  • First put a dressing on open wounds.
  • Assess circulation, sensation, and movement (CSM) before splinting.
  • Splint an injury only if it does not cause more pain. Otherwise, manually immobilize.
  • Splint an injury in the position you find it. Straighten an extremity only if the patient will not receive medical attention for a long time and if circulation has been cut off in extremity by the injury. Follow local protocol.
  • Splint to immobilize the entire injured area from joints above and below the injured area.
  • Use padding that conforms to the space between the splint and the body part.
  • Put splints on both sides of a extremity fracture, if possible.
  • Do not secure the splint on an open wound.
  • Elevate a splinted extremity if possible, but not if it causes pain or may worsen the injury.
  • Apply a cold pack to the injury around the splint; remove after 20 minutes (or 10 minutes if it produces discomfort).
  • Do not completely bandage over fingers or toes, and assess them for CSM frequently after splinting. Swelling, pale or bluish discoloration, tingling or numbness, and cold skin are signs and symptoms of reduced circulation. If any occur, loosen the splint.

STROKE

The FAST Stroke Assessment

F          Face drooping – Is one side of the patient’s face drooping or is it numb? If you ask the person to smile, is his or her smile uneven?

A         Arm weakness – Is one of the patient’s arms weak or numb? If you ask him or her to raise both arms, does one drift downward?

S         Speech difficulty – Is the patient’s speech slurred or is he or she difficulty speaking or understanding? If you ask the patient to repeat a simple sentence, can he or she do it correctly?

T          Time to call EMS – If the patient shows any of these symptoms, immediately ensure help is on the way, and note the time when the symptoms first began.

Signs and Symptoms of Stroke

  • Complaints of sudden, severe headache  
  • Complaints of sudden weakness or decreased or absent sensation in one side of the face, arm, or leg; gait problems  
  • Decreased or absent movement of one or more extremities
  • Facial droop, drooling, inability or difficulty swallowing, tongue deviation
  • Dizziness, confusion  
  • Slurred speech, difficulty speaking and understanding speech  
  • Becoming combative, uncooperative, or restless
  • Double vision or blurred vision
  • Unequal pupils  
  • Vomiting
  • Changing levels of responsiveness or unresponsiveness, possible coma
  • Bladder or bowel incontinence

Emergency Care for Stroke

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Relay your stroke assessment to arriving EMS crew, including the time symptoms began.
  3. Monitor the patient and be prepared to give BLS.
  4. Follow local protocol to administer oxygen if available and you are so trained.
  5. Have the patient lie on his or her back with head and shoulders slightly raised. Loosen a constrictive collar.
  6. If necessary, turn the patient’s head to the side to allow drool or vomit to drain. Use suction if necessary.
  7. Do not let a stroke patient eat or drink anything.
  8. Keep the patient warm and quiet.
  9. Put an unresponsive patient who is breathing normally in the recovery position with the affected side down.

TRIAGE

START Triage Categories

Priority 1—Red—Immediate care needed

Patients with airway and breathing difficulties, uncontrolled or severe bleeding, or decreased mental status

Priority 2—Yellow—Delayed care

Responsive patients with burns without airway problems; major or multiple bone or joint injuries; back injuries with or without spinal cord damage; and responsive patients with other serious injuries without threats to breathing or circulation

Priority 3—Green—Hold care

Responsive patients with minor painful, swollen, deformed extremities, or minor soft-tissue injuries

Priority 4—Black—No care needed

Obviously dead or dying patients

START Triage Assessment

Step 1. Assess Breathing

  • Check whether the patient is breathing normally.
  • If the patient is breathing at rate of below 30 breaths/ minute, move on to assess perfusion or circulation.
  • If the patient is breathing at rate faster than 30 breaths/minute, tag the patient Priority 1 (red tag).
  • If the patient is not breathing, position the head to open the airway.
  • If still not breathing, tag the patient Priority 4 (black tag).
  • If the patient is breathing when the airway is opened, tag the patient Priority 1 (red tag) because the patient needs airway support.

Step 2. Assess Perfusion or Circulation

  • For a patient who is breathing without your having to open the airway, next assess perfusion or circulation.
  • Follow local protocol to check for a pulse or check capillary refill.
  • If there is a pulse or if capillary refill time is less than 2 seconds, move on to assess mental status.
  • If you find no pulse or capillary refill time is longer than 2 seconds, tag the patient Priority 1 (red tag).
  • If the patient is bleeding severely, quickly apply a pressure dressing to control bleeding.

Step 3. Assess Mental Status

  • For a patient who is breathing and has a pulse or capillary refill time under 2 seconds, assess mental status by giving the patient a simple command: “Open your eyes” or “Squeeze my hand.”
  • Patients who follow your command are considered alert and responsive and are tagged Priority 2 (yellow tag).
  • Patients who cannot follow a simple command are considered unresponsive and are tagged Priority 1 (red tag).

VENTILATION

Mouth-to-Mask Ventilation

  1. If the patient has a pulse but is not breathing normally, open the airway and give a breath over 1 second, watching the chest rise and letting it fall.
  2. If the first breath does not go in, try again to open the airway and give another rescue breath. If it still does not go in, the patient may be choking. Proceed to CPR for choking. (See Airway Obstruction in Any Unresponsive Patient, page 59.)
  3. If your breath goes in, continue rescue breathing. Give each breath over 1 second, at rate of 10-12 breaths per minute (1 breath every 5-6 seconds) for an adult; 12-20 breaths per minute for a child or infant (1 breath every 3-5 seconds).
  4. Check the pulse about every 2 minutes. If there is no pulse, provide CPR beginning with chest compressions (pages 45-47.)

Bag Mask (BVM) for Ventilation – 2 Rescuers

  1. Rescuer 1 assembles bag mask with correct size mask and puts the mask over the patient’s mouth and nose.
  2. Rescuer 2 positions hands on the mask: thumbs and index fingers circling each side, other three fingers behind the lower jawbone. Pull jaw up into mask rather than pushing mask down on jaw.
  3. Rescuer 2 opens the airway and seals the mask to the patient’s face.
  4. Rescuer 1 squeezes the bag to provide ventilations:
  • 1 ventilation over 1 second in adult, every 5–6 seconds (or one breath every 6 seconds during CPR if an advanced airway is in place).
  • 1 ventilation over 1 second in child or infant, every 3–5 seconds (or one breath every 6 seconds during CPR if an advanced airway is in place).

WOUNDS AND SOFT-TISSUE INJURIES

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Consider whether wound care requires BSI precautions in addition to gloves. Use eye protection, gown, and other precautions as needed. Wash hands well after care.
  3. Control bleeding by covering the wound with sterile gauze and applying direct pressure.
  4. With minor wounds only, such as abrasions:
  • Irrigate the wound with large amounts of warm or room-temperature water with or without soap to flush out debris and clean the area to prevent infection.
  • Let clean tap water run over the wound until no foreign matter is left in the wound.
  • Never delay or interrupt efforts to stop bleeding to clean a wound.
  • Pat the area dry and apply a sterile dressing and bandage.
  1. Prevent further contamination with a dressing and bandage.
  2. Even with a minor wound, if stitches may be needed or the patient does not have a current tetanus vaccination, ensure that the patient receives medical attention.
  3. Do not let the patient put alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or iodine on the wound, which may damage tissue or cause an allergic reaction.
  4. Avoid breathing or blowing on the wound, which may transmit pathogens.
  5. Do not remove clothing stuck to a wound, but cut around it and leave it in place.
  6. Do not scrub a wound.
