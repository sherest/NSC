Consent

Expressed Consent

  1. Identify yourself to the patient.
  2. State your level of training.
  3. Explain what you think may be wrong.
  4. Describe the care you plan to give and its benefits.
  5. Explain any risks related to care.

Implied Consent

  • Implied for a patient who is unresponsive, confused or seriously injured who cannot give expressed consent for lifesaving care.
  • Applies to children and mentally incompetent adults who need emergency care when a parent or guardian is not present.

When a Victim Refuses Consent

  1. Follow local protocol. When in doubt, err in favor of providing care.
  2. Do not argue with the victim or question the victim’s personal beliefs.
  3. Do not try to give care. Touching a victim who refuses care may make you guilty of battery.
  4. Wait for responding EMS units to arrive and evaluate situation. Do not make independent decision regarding refusal of care.
  5. If possible, have the victim sign a witnessed refusal form or have a witness hear the victim’s refusal.

If an Advance Directive Is Present

  1. Follow local protocol based on state laws.
  2. A DNR order refers only to resuscitative care for victims in cardiac arrest – it does not refer to other treatments.
  3. If there is any doubt, or if a written directive is not present, give care as usual.
  4. Other adults or family members cannot refuse care for victim without a formal advance directive.
  5. If family members ask you to provide care, even when a DNR order is present, it is usually best to give care until responding EMS professionals arrive.
