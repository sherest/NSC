Legal and Ethical Issues

Consent

Expressed Consent

  1. Identify yourself to the patient.
  2. State your level of training.
  3. Explain what you think may be wrong.
  4. Describe the care you plan to give and its benefits.
  5. Explain any risks related to care.

Implied Consent

  • Implied for a patient who is unresponsive, confused or seriously injured who cannot give expressed consent for lifesaving care.
  • Applies to children and mentally incompetent adults who need emergency care when a parent or guardian is not present.

When a Victim Refuses Consent

  1. Follow local protocol. When in doubt, err in favor of providing care.
  2. Do not argue with the victim or question the victim’s personal beliefs.
  3. Do not try to give care. Touching a victim who refuses care may make you guilty of battery.
  4. Wait for responding EMS units to arrive and evaluate situation. Do not make independent decision regarding refusal of care.
  5. If possible, have the victim sign a witnessed refusal form or have a witness hear the victim’s refusal.

If an Advance Directive Is Present

  1. Follow local protocol based on state laws.
  2. A DNR order refers only to resuscitative care for victims in cardiac arrest – it does not refer to other treatments.
  3. If there is any doubt, or if a written directive is not present, give care as usual.
  4. Other adults or family members cannot refuse care for victim without a formal advance directive.
  5. If family members ask you to provide care, even when a DNR order is present, it is usually best to give care until responding EMS professionals arrive.

Negligence Occurs When 4 Conditions Occur:

  • You have duty to act. Once you begin treatment, you have a duty to act.
  • You fail to give care or to meet standards of care, whether willfully (gross negligence) or unintentionally.
  • Patient suffers injury or damage, including emotional or psychological damage.
  • Patient’s injury or damage resulted from your actions or inactions, for which you can be sued (proximate cause).

Confidentiality

  • Applies to previous medical problems, physical problems, medication use and all other patient information.
  • Assessment findings and emergency care given are confidential.
  • You have an ethical responsibility to respect the patient’s right to privacy.
  • Never share patient information with others, including family members and coworkers.

Reportable Events

  • Emergency medical responders are obligated to report certain situations to appropriate authorities:
    • Child, elder and spouse abuse or domestic violence
    • Certain crimes, such as gunshot and knife wounds, suspicious burns, rape and sexual assault
    • Vehicle crashes
    • Certain infectious diseases
    • Exposure to patient’s body fluids

Falsification of Records

  • Errors of omission or commission must be documented.
  • Any intentional omission or misreporting in written documentation is illegal and unethical.

(See also Correcting Errors under Documentation Tips)

Ethical Responsibilities

  • As an emergency medical responder, you accept ethical responsibility to provide the best care possible.
  • Make the physical and emotional needs of the patient your highest priority.
  • Practice your caregiving skills until you are certain you have mastered all skills.
  • Regularly attend continuing education and refresher programs to learn new information and skills and to ensure continued mastery of skills.
  • After each patient care episode, review your performances to see if you can improve your response time, give better care, or communicate more effectively.
  • Be honest in your reporting and documentation.
