Medications

Aspirin for Heart Attack

  • Many patients at risk take one low-dose aspirin daily unless they are allergic or have side effects.  
  • For patients who do not need to avoid aspirin, aspirin is recommended when experiencing heart attack symptoms
  • Follow local protocol to encourage the patient to chew one adult aspirin (325 mg) or 2 to 4 low-dose baby aspirin (81 mg each).

Bronchodilator (Inhaler) for an Asthma Attack

  1. Follow local protocol to assist the patient in using asthma medication (usually a metered dose inhaler) when:
    • Patient confirms it is an asthma attack.
    • Patient identifies the inhaler as his or her prescribed asthma medication.
    • Patient cannot self-administer the medication.
  2. Help the patient rest in position for easiest breathing.
  3. If needed, patient may use the inhaler again as prescribed or directed by his or her medical provider.

Emergency Epinephrine Auto-Injector for Severe Allergic Reaction

Follow local protocol to assist the patient in using an EpiPen or EpiPen Jr., AnaKit, or TwinJect.

  1. The auto-injector is removed from its case and the protective cover is removed.
  2. The tip is pushed firmly into outer thigh, causing the medication to be injected.
  3. Tip is held in place while the medication is injected (follow the product instructions for how long). Then the injector is removed and disposed of properly in a biohazard container.
  4. Effects of the emergency epinephrine generally last 15 to 20 minutes.
  5. Patient may experience side effects of epinephrine: fast heartbeat, breathing difficulty, nausea and vomiting, dizziness or nervousness and headache.
  6. If symptoms continue, and if EMS personnel are not expected within 5-10 minutes, administer a second dose of epinephrine, using a second auto-injector.

Glucose Paste for Hypoglycemia

  • Use oral glucose paste or gel, if you are so trained, for a patient in a diabetic emergency with mild altered mental status who can still swallow and is not snoring or gagging.
  1. Squeeze a small amount of paste onto a tongue depressor.
  2. Carefully spread paste between cheek and gums, where it will be quickly absorbed.
  3. Continue to administer small doses until all of tube has been used (or follow local protocol for dose to administer).

Nitroglycerin for a Cardiac Emergency

  • Increases blood flow through partially restricted arteries.
  • Generally prescribed for angina caused by narrowed coronary arteries.
  • Forms include:
      • Tablets dissolved under the tongue  
      • Tablets that dissolve in the cheek  
      • Extended-release capsules  
      • Oral sprays  
      • Extended-release patches applied to the chest
  1. Follow local protocol and the instructions on the medication to help with the drug.
  2. Patient should be seated because dizziness or fainting may occur.
  3. Do not give a drug to an unresponsive patient.
