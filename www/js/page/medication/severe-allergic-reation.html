Emergency Epinephrine Auto-Injector for Severe Allergic Reaction

Follow local protocol to assist the patient in using an EpiPen or EpiPen Jr., AnaKit, or TwinJect.

  1. The auto-injector is removed from its case and the protective cover is removed.
  2. The tip is pushed firmly into outer thigh, causing the medication to be injected.
  3. Tip is held in place while the medication is injected (follow the product instructions for how long). Then the injector is removed and disposed of properly in a biohazard container.
  4. Effects of the emergency epinephrine generally last 15 to 20 minutes.
  5. Patient may experience side effects of epinephrine: fast heartbeat, breathing difficulty, nausea and vomiting, dizziness or nervousness and headache.
  6. If symptoms continue, and if EMS personnel are not expected within 5-10 minutes, administer a second dose of epinephrine, using a second auto-injector.

Glucose Paste for Hypoglycemia

  • Use oral glucose paste or gel, if you are so trained, for a patient in a diabetic emergency with mild altered mental status who can still swallow and is not snoring or gagging.
  1. Squeeze a small amount of paste onto a tongue depressor.
  2. Carefully spread paste between cheek and gums, where it will be quickly absorbed.
  3. Continue to administer small doses until all of tube has been used (or follow local protocol for dose to administer).
