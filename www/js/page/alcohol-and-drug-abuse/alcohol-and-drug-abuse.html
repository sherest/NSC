1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. For responsive patient:
    • Stay with patient and protect from injury (take away car keys).
    • Do not let patient lie down on the back.
    • Care for injuries.
    • For responsive patient known to have taken a drug or other substance, follow local protocol to call the Poison Control Center and follow instructions.
  3. For unresponsive patient:
    • Position patient in recovery position; be prepared for vomiting.
    • Monitor breathing and provide BLS if necessary.
    • In cold environment, intoxicated patient is likely to experience hypothermia. Keep patient warm.
  4. For injured intoxicated patient:
    • Do not rely on patient’s perception of injury to guide care because alcohol may keep patient from feeling pain.
    • Give emergency care as if patient were unresponsive, based on signs of injury or illness rather than reported symptoms.
    • If mechanism of injury suggests possible spinal injury, do not move patient but keep head aligned with the body.
  5. Provide care for any condition that occurs (e.g., seizures, shock, cardiac arrest).
  6. Keep patient from harming self or others.
  7. Do not try to induce vomiting, which may cause further harm and is unlikely to help the patient.
  8. Alcohol and some drugs make some people hostile and violent. Stay away and call law enforcement if violence threatens.
  9. When illegal drugs are involved, protect the crime scene.

Opioid Drug Overdose

  1. Perform standard patient care.
  2. Provide the general care for drug abuse and overdose emergencies.
  3. Try to learn what drug was taken and activate EMS (if you are not part of the EMS response) Call immediately for an unresponsive patient.
  4. If possible, administer naloxone to the patient to counteract the opioid drug’s effects.
    • Naloxone is the drug in an emergency overdose kit (such as Narcan).
    • A naloxone emergency kit may be present at the scene, or the patient’s family may have or know of a nearby emergency kit.
    • Naloxone may be administered intramuscularly via an auto-injector (like an emergency epinephrine auto-injector for a severe allergic reaction) or intranasally via a nasal device.
    • To administer the drug, always follow the instructions on the kit.
    • Additional doses may be required if the patient’s signs do not improve before additional emergency personnel arrive.
    • Note that you do not have to be absolutely certain that the patient has taken an opioid drug, because naloxone will not harm a patient with these signs and symptoms due to some other cause.
