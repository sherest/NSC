Applying Dressings

  • Wash hands and put on gloves.
  • Choose a dressing larger than the wound. Do not touch the dressing where it will contact the wound.
  • Carefully lay (not slide) the dressing on the wound.
  • If blood seeps through, do not remove dressing but add more dressings on top of it.
  • Follow local protocol for use of hemostatic dressings.
  • Apply direct pressure as needed to control bleeding. Apply a bandage to hold the dressing in place and maintain pressure.

Guidelines for Bandaging

  • Bandages do not directly touch the wound, and should be clean but not necessarily sterile.
  • To put pressure on a wound to stop bleeding or to prevent swelling of an injury, apply the bandage firmly â€“ but do not cut off circulation. The pressure is sufficient if the bandage is snug but you can slip a finger under it.
  • Never encircle the neck with a bandage.
  • Do not cover fingers or toes unless injured.
  • With a bandage around a limb, check fingers or toes for color, warmth, and sensation (normal touch, not tingling) to ensure circulation is not cut off.
  • If signs of reduced circulation, unwrap the bandage and reapply it less tightly.
  • Because swelling may continue, keep checking tightness of bandage to prevent cutting off circulation.
  • Be sure bandage is secure enough that dressing will not move and expose the wound.
  • With elastic and roller bandages, anchor first end and tie, tape, pin, or clip the ending section in place.
  • Use an elastic bandage to make a pressure bandage around a limb to control bleeding and protect the wound.
  • Use an elastic bandage to support a joint and prevent swelling.
  • Wrap a bandage from the bottom of the limb upward to help prevent cutting off circulation.
  • Bandage joint in the position where it will stay.
  • Use a wide bandage with evenly distributed pressure around the extremity.
