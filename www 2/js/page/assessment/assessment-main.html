Assessment

Primary Assessment

Assess Responsiveness

  • State your name and say that you are an emergency medical responder here to help. Ask the patient what is wrong.
  • If patient is not speaking, making other sounds, or moving, tap patient gently on shoulder and shout, "Are you OK?"
  • Be careful not to move patient. Do not shake the shoulder or touch head or neck.
  • Always suspect spinal injury in a trauma patient and take steps to stabilize patient's head and neck.
  • For unresponsive patient, immediately ensure EMS has been activated.

Look for Normal Breathing While Checking for a Pulse (less than 10 seconds)

  • Reflex gasping (agonal respirations) just after cardiac arrest is not normal breathing.
  • In a responsive adult or child, check the radial pulse.
  • In an unresponsive adult or child, check the carotid pulse.
  • In an infant, use the brachial pulse.

Check for Serious Bleeding

  • Control serious bleeding immediately with direct pressure.

Secondary Assessment

  • SAMPLE history
  • Vital signs

SAMPLE History

S
Signs and symptoms. What can you observe about the patient (signs)? Ask how he or she feels (symptoms) and to describe any pain felt. Ask why EMS was called.
A
Allergies. Ask the patient about any allergies to foods, medicines, insect stings, anything in the environment or other substances. Look for a medical alert ID.
M
Medications. Ask if patient is currently taking or recently has taken any prescribed medications or over-the-counter products, including vitamins, supplements and herbal remedies.
P
Pertinent past medical history. Ask if patient has experienced anything like this before, is seeing a health care provider for any illnesses or has ever been in the hospital. Check for a medical alert ID.
L
Last food or drink. Ask what the patient last ate or drank, and when and how much.
E
Events. Ask what happened and when, and try to identify events leading to the current situation.

Vital Signs

Measuring Blood Pressure by Auscultation

  1. Position the cuff on the patient's arm.
  2. Position the stethoscope diaphragm and inflate the cuff 30 mmHg beyond where the tapping sound disappears.
  3. Watching the gauge, slowly deflate cuff at 2-3 mmHg per second.
  4. Note the pressure when the pulse tapping sound returns (systolic pressure).
  5. As the cuff deflates, note the reading when the tapping sound disappears (diastolic pressure).
  6. Deflate the cuff and record the pressure (systolic/diastolic).

Measuring Blood Pressure by Palpation

If you do not have a stethoscope or the scene is too noisy, measure systolic blood pressure by palpation.

  1. While palpating the radial pulse, inflate the cuff 30 mmHg beyond the point where you stop feeling the pulse.
  2. While watching the gauge, open the valve to slowly deflate the cuff.
  3. Note the pressure when you feel radial pulse return (systolic pressure). You cannot measure diastolic pressure by palpation.
  4. Record the pressure as systolic pressure and include the word palpated (e.g., "130 palpated" or "130/P").

Physical Examination

Check each area for DOTS/ DCAP-BTLS

(See Abbreviations)

  1. Palpate and inspect the scalp and face.
  2. Check for eye injuries and assess the pupils.
  3. Check ears and nose for bleeding or leaking fluid.
  4. Check mouth for injury, bleeding, and potential airway obstructions.
  5. Remove upper body clothing and palpate and inspect the neck in front and back.
  6. Palpate and inspect the chest, and observe movement with breathing.
  7. Palpate and inspect abdominal quadrants.
  8. Reach under patient to palpate the back and check for bleeding.
  9. Remove lower clothing and palpate pelvis.
  10. Palpate and inspect each upper and lower leg.
  11. Check the pulse in each foot.
  12. Check each foot for movement and sensation.
  13. Palpate and inspect upper extremities from clavicle to shoulder and down the arm.
  14. Check radial pulse in each arm.
  15. Check hands for movement and sensation.

Reassessment

  • Continue to assess the patient while awaiting additional EMS resources and giving care.
  • Calm and reassure the patient while reassessing breathing and circulation and repeating the vital signs and physical examination as needed.
  • Repeat reassessments as indicated by the patientâ€™s condition or:
    • Every 15 minutes for a stable patient
    • Every 5 minutes for an unstable patient

Compare reassessments results to first assessments (baseline status):

  • Level of responsiveness
  • Airway maintenance
  • Adequacy of breathing (rate, depth, effort)
  • Adequacy of circulation (carotid or radial pulse; skin color, temperature, and moisture)
  • Chief complaint (pain remains the same, getting worse, or getting better)
  • Presence of new or previously undisclosed symptoms
